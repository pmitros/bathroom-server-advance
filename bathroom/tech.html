Technology

To put together the bathroom server required a unique mix of engineering and good ol' fashioned Southern know-how. A unique blend of MIT mechanical, electronic, civil and computer engineers came together to build this product.

Random's Riad Wahby forges a raw piece of silicon into a next-generation 128 bit CPU for the bathroom server

Detecting bathroom use

Each bathroom is installed with a robotic head. The robot's combination of sensors and artificial intelligence allows it to detect when a bathroom is in use in real-time. The video is then transmitted to a Cray XMP supercomputer for further analysis and refinement of the robotic system. To ensure privacy, we use sophisticated cryptography techniques to ensure that only a small select group of trusted individual have access (Andrei, Shmike and Robert), who are trusted not to abuse it.

Tracing back room locations

When viewed internally from the dorm, the bathroom server displays the path from the user's room to the nearest empty bathroom. The first step towards doing this is to determine the user's room.

To do this, we have five servers located around the dorm. Each of these servers sends a ping packet to the client machine. The client machine responds with a pong packet. The time between when the ping is sent and when the pong is received tells us how long it takes the packets to travel through the ether in the dorm (ether is a substance that permeates all of space. It was postulated in the 1500s, discounted in the early 1900s, and then proven to exist with the creation of ethernet in the 1970s). The distances to the servers let us triangulate the approximate positions of the machines in the dorm. We also used more advanced packet interferometry techniques that are beyond the scope of this document.

Bright readers might observe that we only need three servers to triangulate a location in 3d. While correct, we have two redundant servers for added robustness and accuracy. Sadly, fluctuations in the ether may distort out readings in much the same way as temperature layers in the water distort sonar measurements. If we misread your room, please e-mail us, and we will recalibrate our equipment (ether is fairly stationary, so the fix should be fairly permanent.

Plotting shortest path

Normally, finding the closest bathroom and finding the path to it is an NP-hard problem (computationally very difficult), and we would not have been able to compute it in real-time on our server.

To solve this problem quickly and easily, we rely on Kirkoff's Current Law, which states that electricity always takes the shortest path between two points. We run a small current from one bathroom location to another, and use magnetics to trace back the electron flow.
